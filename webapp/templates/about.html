About EvoDraughts

What is EvoDraughts?

EvoDraughts is my Final Year Project where I'm using Grammatical Evolution (GE) to evolve draughts strategies. I built this using the GRAPE framework (Grammatical Algorithms in Python for Evolution) which lets me evolve AI strategies through genetic algorithms instead of manually programming them.

How It Works

1

Grammar Definition

I wrote a Backus-Naur Form (BNF) grammar that defines how strategies can be built. This includes things like conditional statements, math operations, and ways to check board features. Each strategy is basically a program written in this grammar.

2

Evolution Process

I start with a population of random strategies and let them evolve over generations. Each strategy plays games against random opponents, and I measure their fitness based on how many games they win. The ones that perform better are more likely to survive and reproduce.

3

Strategy Selection

I use genetic algorithm operations - selection, crossover (combining two strategies), and mutation (randomly changing parts). Over many generations, the strategies get better at playing draughts. I've seen strategies go from random moves to actually making decent decisions.

4

Human Testing

This web app lets people play against the evolved strategies. It's really useful for me to see how they perform against human players, not just random opponents. Plus it makes the project more interactive and fun for testing.

Technical Details

Grammatical Evolution

This is a type of genetic programming where I represent strategies as binary strings that get mapped to valid expressions through my grammar. It's cool because it guarantees the strategies are always syntactically correct.

GRAPE Framework

I'm using the GRAPE Python framework which handles a lot of the grammatical evolution mechanics for me. It takes care of turning binary genomes into phenotypes (the actual strategy expressions) and manages the evolution process.

Board Representation

When a strategy needs to evaluate a position, it gets access to board features like piece positions, how many pieces each player has, king counts, mobility (available moves), and center control. The strategy uses these to decide what move to make.

Fitness Evaluation

I evaluate each strategy by having it play multiple games against random opponents. The fitness score is based on how many games it wins. Lower fitness means better performance (I'm minimizing losses). I also add a small penalty for overly simple strategies to encourage complexity.

Difficulty Levels

I've organized the strategies into three difficulty levels by manually assigning specific evolved strategies to each level:

  • Easy: Strategies that are easier to play against, good if you're just learning or want a casual game.
  • Medium: Strategies that offer a balanced challenge for intermediate players.
  • Hard: The strongest evolved strategies that should give experienced players a decent challenge.

Rather than automatically categorizing strategies based on fitness scores, I manually selected and assigned specific strategy files to each difficulty level. This allows me to ensure a better gameplay experience and properly test strategies based on their actual performance against human players.
